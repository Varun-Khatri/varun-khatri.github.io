What 10+ Years of Building Mobile and VR Games Taught Me About Performance and Scale

Game Development January 19 2026

When I wrote my first line of Unity code over a decade ago, my goal was simple: make the game work.

Today, after shipping mobile games with over 100 million downloads and building performance-critical systems for large-scale VR projects, my definition of “working” has changed completely.

This post is a reflection on lessons learned from developing games across mobile, VR, and experimental platforms—and how those lessons reshaped the way I approach performance, systems design, and technical ownership.

Performance Is Not an Optimization Phase

Early in my career, performance was something you fixed later. On mobile—especially in live-service titles—that mindset does not survive long.

When you are targeting low-end devices, wide GPU variance, and long player sessions, performance decisions must be made at feature design time.

  • Allocations must be intentional
  • Shaders and VFX must be budgeted systems
  • Performance tooling must exist early

In VR, this becomes even more critical. Miss your frame budget and the player feels it immediately.

Scale Changes Everything

A save system or UI flow feels trivial—until millions of players use it.

At scale, edge cases are not rare; they are guaranteed. Systems must survive:

  • Corrupted data
  • Partial updates
  • Backward compatibility requirements

Over time, I learned to design systems not for elegance in isolation, but for resilience in production.

Technical Art Is a Force Multiplier

Leaning into technical art—shaders, VFX, rendering—was one of the most impactful shifts in my career.

Owning this space allowed me to bridge art and engineering, prototype faster, and maintain visual quality without sacrificing performance.

Tools like Shader Graph and VFX Graph enabled scalable visual systems that were controllable, measurable, and production-ready.

Data-Oriented Thinking Changes Architecture

Working with Unity DOTS reshaped how I approach performance-critical systems. Even outside DOTS, the principles apply:

  • Data layout matters
  • Cache-friendly systems scale better
  • Behavior should follow data, not the other way around

Ownership Matters More Than Titles

The developers who made the biggest impact were not defined by titles, but by ownership.

Ownership means thinking beyond tasks—anticipating downstream effects, building tools for others, and designing systems that outlive individual features.

Closing Thoughts

Game development at scale is less about clever tricks and more about discipline, foresight, and empathy for future developers—including yourself.

This blog will be a place where I share lessons, breakdowns, and experiments from that journey.
